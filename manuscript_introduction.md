# FlowMind: A Multi-Agent AI System for Continuous Flow Chemistry Process Design

## Introduction — First Draft (preprint format)

> Citations are placeholders (`[ref]`, `[refs]`) for direct insertion. System name: **FlowMind**.

---

Continuous flow chemistry has matured into a workhorse technology for pharmaceutical, fine-chemical, and on-demand manufacturing. Its advantages over batch are by now well-documented: orders-of-magnitude improvements in heat and mass transfer through high surface-to-volume ratios, intrinsically safer handling of hazardous intermediates, access to short residence times, and ready accommodation of photochemistry, electrochemistry, gas–liquid regimes, and multi-step coupling [refs]. The hardware has matured; commercial pumps, mixers, photoreactors, and back-pressure regulators are widely available. Adoption, however, is gated less by what flow can do than by what it costs to *design*. The gap between a published batch protocol and a working flow process is routinely closed by a small team over weeks of iteration. The bottleneck has shifted from running flow chemistry to designing flow processes.

Designing a flow process is not a single decision; it is a chain of interlocking decisions across several engineering domains, and each decision constrains the next. The kinetics question — what residence time τ delivers the target conversion at flow temperature, and what intensification factor is realistic — relies on knowledge that is rarely present in the original batch protocol. The fluid-dynamics question — what tube diameter keeps flow laminar but Péclet-adequate at the chosen flow rate, and what pressure drop ΔP the pump can sustain — depends on the kinetics answer, the solvent properties, and the available hardware. The mass-transfer question — whether mixing time is shorter than reaction time (Da_mass < 1) — is set by the diameter. The heat-transfer question — whether the heat generated at peak rate is removable through the tube wall (Da_th < 1) — is set by the diameter, the reaction enthalpy, and the cooling configuration. The phase regime, the back-pressure regulator setting, the tubing material under the proposed solvent and temperature, the deoxygenation strategy, the inline quench, and the multi-step coupling chemistry are all coupled to one another. In industrial practice this work is divided among a synthetic chemist, a reaction engineer, and a safety reviewer, who iterate over weeks. For an individual chemist attempting to translate a batch protocol into a defensible flow design, the design surface is forbidding.

The community has built tools that address parts of this design surface, but no single tool covers it. Manual literature transfer remains the dominant method: a chemist reads analogous flow papers and adapts them, a process limited by recall and biased toward whichever precedents are most readily remembered. Classical process simulators [refs] solve the engineering rigorously, but they require an already-specified flowsheet, do not reason about chemistry, and have a learning curve unsuited to early-stage process exploration. Bayesian and active-learning optimisers for flow [refs] are effective at refining a known process under known apparatus, but they cannot propose a flow process from a chemistry brief — they need a process to optimise. Cheminformatics retrosynthesis tools [refs] answer *what* to make; they do not answer *how* to run it in flow. A complementary body of knowledge sits in canonical flow-chemistry handbooks and reviews [refs], which encode rules of thumb for solvent choice, material compatibility, intensification factors by reaction class, mixing strategy, and unit-operation selection. This handbook knowledge is what an experienced flow chemist actually uses — but it is dispersed across textbook chapters and review articles, qualitative in form, and almost never operationalised into a representation a computer can apply consistently. The gap is therefore clear: no current tool designs a flow process from a chemistry brief while honouring the handbook-level fundamentals the field already trusts.

The recent generation of large-language-model (LLM) copilots for chemistry [refs] has substantially expanded what an AI assistant can do for a chemist: synthesis planning, autonomous batch experimentation, literature reasoning, and natural-language interaction with laboratory instrumentation. These systems are an important advance. They are also, with few exceptions, batch-oriented, single-agent, and engineering-blind. A monolithic LLM asked for a flow design must hold synthetic-chemistry, reaction-kinetics, fluid-mechanics, heat-transfer, and process-safety expertise simultaneously in working memory; the result is that every domain is diluted. Anyone who has asked a frontier LLM for a flow design has seen the characteristic failure modes — a Reynolds number reported without a velocity, a back-pressure regulator selected without a vapour-pressure check, a (V_R, τ, Q) triple that fails V_R = τ·Q on a calculator, a "validated" design without an audit trail. Single-shot LLM design lacks the cross-checking, structured disagreement, and deterministic engineering grounding that catch these errors before they reach the user.

The structure of flow process design — a coupled, multi-domain decision problem solved by a small team of complementary specialists — maps directly onto a multi-agent architecture in which each agent owns one engineering domain and a separate orchestrator integrates their judgments. This is not an aesthetic choice but a structural one. Domain ownership prevents scope drift; cross-agent audit catches the arithmetic and unit errors that single-shot LLMs reliably produce; bounded revision allows the system to refine a design rather than replace it; an explicit selection step forces the system to commit to a defensible final design rather than to produce a confident average. The architecture mirrors how industrial flow-development teams actually work, and it admits a clean division of labour between two kinds of reasoning: judgments that require *chemical and engineering meaning* (which mechanism is operating, whether a given intermediate is light- or oxygen-sensitive, whether a tubing material will survive a particular solvent at temperature) and *computations that require physics* (Reynolds, Péclet, Damköhler, pressure drop, vapour pressure). The first kind is what LLM agents do well; the second is what closed-form equations and a curated rule base do well. The organising principle of this work is therefore: **LLMs interpret chemistry; deterministic code, anchored in handbook fundamentals, computes the engineering numbers.**

We introduce **FlowMind**, to our knowledge the first multi-agent AI pipeline purpose-built for continuous flow chemistry process design. FlowMind is not a batch-to-flow translator; it is a flow-chemistry design assistant that takes a chemistry brief — a batch protocol, a target reaction, or a research goal — and returns a defensible, intensification-aware flow process design accompanied by a process-flow diagram, traceable engineering calculations, literature analogies, handbook-grounded rule checks, and explicit uncertainty flags. FlowMind is built around the chemist's workflow: paste a protocol, receive a design that is ready for laboratory validation rather than a polished but unsafe paper exercise. The system is intended for the working flow chemist as a co-pilot, not as a black-box autonomous design engine, and every quantitative output is auditable back to the equation, rule, or chemistry decision that produced it.

FlowMind comprises five tightly coupled stages. First, a chemistry-interpretation agent reads the brief and produces a structured chemistry plan capturing the mechanism, reaction class, oxygen and moisture sensitivities, stream logic (which reagents must be co-fed and which must be kept apart), quench logic, and an explicit intensification mandate stating why and by how much flow should improve on batch. Second, a plan-aware retrieval stage queries a curated flow-chemistry corpus for analogies whose mechanism and reaction class match the chemistry plan, returning the precedents an experienced chemist would actually cite. Third, a nine-step deterministic engineering calculator carries the design from batch conditions through kinetics and residence time, reactor sizing, fluid dynamics, pressure drop, mass transfer, heat transfer, back-pressure regulator sizing, and process metrics, with every quantity traceable to a closed-form equation. The calculator is grounded in a knowledge base of approximately 2,500 flow-chemistry design rules extracted from established flow-chemistry literature [refs], which constrain solvent compatibility, tubing material selection, intensification factors by reaction class, and unit-operation selection. Fourth, a multi-agent ENGINE Council — a Designer that generates candidate designs, four domain specialists (Dr. Chemistry, Dr. Kinetics, Dr. Fluidics, Dr. Safety), a Skeptic that audits arithmetic and scope, and a Chief Engineer that selects the winner — scores each candidate, audits its internal consistency, refines it under bounded domain authority, and selects a final design via a weighted score that includes an explicit Process Value Score quantifying how much the proposed flow process actually improves on batch. Fifth, a chemistry-aware process flow diagram is rendered automatically from the validated topology. The headline guarantee is that every number on the final process diagram traces back to a closed-form equation, a handbook rule, or an explicit chemistry decision — no number is hallucinated.

Six design principles distinguish FlowMind from a generic LLM wrapper. *(i) Deterministic-physics primacy*: LLM agents never invent engineering numbers; the nine-step calculator owns Reynolds, pressure drop, reactor volume, residence time, Damköhler numbers, and BPR pressure. *(ii) Handbook-grounded rule base*: material compatibility, intensification factors, unit-operation selection, and safety heuristics are sourced from a structured rule base extracted from established flow-chemistry literature [refs], not from the latent memory of an LLM. *(iii) Intensification-first, not feasibility-first*: a flow design that does not measurably outperform the corresponding batch process is rejected or downgraded to a screening hypothesis rather than labelled as validated. *(iv) Explicit uncertainty handling*: when literature analogies disagree with class-level kinetics, or when a kinetic anchor is judged unreliable, FlowMind returns a screening recommendation rather than a false validation. *(v) Domain-bounded agent authority*: each council agent may only edit fields within its own domain — Dr. Kinetics owns residence time; Dr. Fluidics owns tube diameter; Dr. Safety owns BPR setting and tubing material; Dr. Chemistry owns concentration — preventing scope drift and inter-agent contamination. *(vi) Plan-aware retrieval*: literature analogies are surfaced through semantic search keyed to the chemistry plan rather than through naive keyword matching, so the precedents the system uses are mechanistically appropriate.

This work makes the following contributions. We present FlowMind, to our knowledge the first multi-agent AI pipeline purpose-built for continuous flow chemistry process design, and we describe a hybrid LLM–deterministic architecture in which language-model agents reason about chemistry while a nine-step engineering calculator and a handbook-derived rule base own all engineering computation. We introduce an explicit, computable IntensificationMandate that converts the qualitative question "is flow better than batch?" into a quantitative design gate, and we frame domain-bounded multi-agent deliberation as a mechanism for catching the failure modes of single-shot LLM design. We benchmark FlowMind on a 3 × 3 matrix of upstream and council models drawn from frontier (Claude Sonnet, GPT-4o) and economical (GPT-4o-mini) commercial endpoints, complemented by a local-model stress test using Gemma to characterise behaviour under fully self-hosted deployment. We report an ablation study comparing the full multi-agent council against a single-shot LLM proposal under matched conditions, providing quantitative evidence that the multi-agent architecture contributes measurable design quality rather than only measurable cost. Finally, we present three end-to-end case studies — an isoxazole 1,3-dipolar cycloaddition, an SNAr substitution, and a photoredox process — that exhibit the full design output, the council deliberation log, and the trade-offs surfaced by the system.
